# Attention as the scarce resource
The question you brought was: how do you know when a thing is worth it? Joe's split was between money worth it and attention worth it. Money buys a lot, but it does not buy you out of the worry that earned it. When something takes an outsized share of your attention and is hard to put down, the move is not automatically to shut it down. Refactor how you engage with it first. Automate, delegate, get help, change the shape of the thing. Shutting it down stays on the table, but it is one option among several.

# Keeping outside stress out of work
You asked how to keep something big happening outside work from pulling your attention while you are working.

- You will not do this perfectly. The people who build clean walls between the parts of their life tend to be in specialized fields, and it is not the normal case.
- The most effective move Joe knows is to make a deal with yourself. Decide, explicitly, that you are not going to worry about the thing right now, and trust that you will come back to it later. Deciding is the part that works. Trying to suppress the thought is not.
- Reduce the reminders. This is digital hygiene: what notifications arrive when, which accounts and inboxes you look at during which parts of the day. It does not mean never checking your phone at work. It does mean not letting a sportsbook push notifications at you during a work day, because that mixes two things you do not want mixed.

# Getting the most out of a client visit
You asked what you should be watching and taking notes on when you go to the client site.

- Be inquisitive. Questions are good and most people enjoy someone being curious about what they do. One of the real perks of this job is getting to look inside a lot of different industries.
- Do not ask so many questions that the meeting's purpose gets derailed. Meg and Nick have a long relationship with this client and are going in with an agenda. Let them drive.

- Aim to come across as professional, curious, and interested. That is an opening a new person on the team gets to use.

# Story points and estimation
Meg pointed you at Joe after a backlog session with Jason where points were being used as days. See [[Story Points]], [[Relative Estimation using Story Points]], and [[Planning Poker]] for the full treatment. What came up here:

- There are several units teams estimate in. Calendar time, active working time, T-shirt sizes, and story points. Each has a use. Calendar time answers the client's real question, which is when the thing will be done. Active time tells you how much work there is, and you can subtract vacation and holidays to get calendar time from it.
- Story points measure relative size. They are intentionally unitless, and the scale is relative rather than absolute, so every team's points mean something slightly different.
- Because they describe the work and not the worker, a five point story should take you longer than it takes Nick. He has fifteen years on you. That is the point. The estimate does not change based on who picks it up.
- Planning poker is the usual mechanic. Pick a story that feels small, ideally half a day to a day of work, call it one point, and pin it up as the reference. Then hold every other story against it. Twice as big is a two. The modified Fibonacci sequence means four is not an option, so a story four times the size gets a five.
- Track how many points you complete per sprint and average it over a window. That is velocity. Once you have velocity in the rearview mirror, you can project the rest of an estimated backlog forward.
- The reason teams avoid days is miscommunication. Say "six days" and forget the word "active," and the client hears calendar days and expects it Saturday. Both of you do that math silently and neither of you notices. Story points take time out of the sentence.
- Estimating your own work is worth doing regardless. It forces you to think the problem through beforehand, and comparing your estimates to your tracked time tells you how optimistic you run. If you know you are optimistic by 2x, you can correct for it. For personal estimates, active hours makes more sense than points. See [[Estimation Calibration]].
- Joe's caveat: relative complexity made a lot of sense when writing code was slow. Now that you can prototype and iterate fast, and are often directing the work rather than typing it, teams are actively rethinking estimation. That does not make it less useful. It means the practice is in flux.

[[Agile Estimation and Planning|Agile Estimating and Planning]] by [[Mike Cohn]] is the reference. Joe suggests skimming it for the vocabulary rather than reading it cover to cover.

# Recovering lost work in Git
You lost a chunk of your learning backlog and terms when you pushed and pulled in the [[TKM]] vault. We fixed it live.

The diagnosis first. Recovery depends on how you lost it. If a commit existed, [[Git Reflog|the reflog]] can find it, and a graph client makes that much easier to flip through than the command line. If you never committed locally, it may simply be gone.

The path we took, using [[Gitup]]:

1. Install GitUp's command line tool while we were in there. It is handy.
2. Look at the changes view and sort out which files actually belong in the TKM. Some of what was uncommitted did not.
3. Realize the confusion was between the vault and its submodule, and open the right repository. See [[Git Submodules]] and [[How to handle git submodules]].
4. Commit the current state so nothing is at risk.
5. Make a [[Backup branches|backup branch]] without checking it out. In GitUp that is `B`.
6. Check out main and [[Cherry-Picking|cherry-pick]] the commit holding the missing work.
7. Resolve the merge conflict in the learning backlog. We opened the vault in VS Code, accepted both sides, then cleaned up the duplicate lines by hand and removed the conflict markers.
8. Commit, pull, then push. This needed a force push because of the rewrite, which is acceptable here because the backup branch existed and this is your own vault.
9. Afterward, `git stash --include-untracked`, then `git pull --rebase`, then `git stash apply`, so you keep your uncommitted work while taking in the remote changes. See [[git stash]] and [[Keeping a clean git history with pull rebase]].

Two habits to take away:
- For a personal vault, automate snapshot commits on a cron job. You are committing to have a restore point, not to build a readable history, so there is no reason to do it by hand.
- Keep personal material in a separate [[Obsidian Vault|Obsidian vault]]. Atomic technically has access to the TKM vault. Nobody is reading over your shoulder, but you should know that before you put gambling notes in it. Creating a second vault is one menu item in Obsidian.

# Terminal and instrumental goal analysis
For the "what do I value" question, Joe walked you through [[Terminal and Instrumental Goals|terminal and instrumental goal analysis]]. It sounds fancy and is not. Write down a goal. Ask why until you hit something you want for no further reason, which is a terminal goal. Ask how in the other direction to find the instrumental goals that get you there. See [[Terminal and Instrumental Goal Map]] and [[Abstraction Laddering]], which uses the same ladder for problems instead of values.

We ran it on two of your goals.

Making money. Asking why produced three answers. It is fun. It works as a scoreboard more than as spending power, since you do not actually spend much. And it buys security: if everything blew up tomorrow you could live for thirty years. Asking how produced having a job, keeping that job, and not doing anything complicated with the money you already have.

Becoming a better developer. Why produced several: it makes you feel like a valuable human being, it lets you build tools that improve your life, it is fun, and it is a way to solve interesting problems and learn how things work. You described that last one as peeling back a good magic trick. How produced learning, building things, and practicing.

Joe's note on reading the results: sometimes a terminal goal is just an emotion with nothing under it, and sometimes it has real history beneath it. Someone who wants financial security badly often grew up without it, or had a role model who had it. The why side is where the values question gets answered, and it is probably worth spending more time there.

> [!INFO] Factual Clarifications and Learning Opportunities
> A few things from this conversation are worth a second look. A language model wrote this section, so it is itself subject to fact checking.
> 1. Epicurus is a rough fit for the hedonism point. He argued for modest, sustainable pleasure and freedom from disturbance, and warned against excess specifically because it backfires. The Cyrenaics, following Aristippus, are the closer match for pursuing pleasure to the fullest. The underlying point that optimizing for happiness alone tends to hollow out still holds.
> 2. The book is *Agile Estimating and Planning* by Mike Cohn. The note is filed here as [[Agile Estimation and Planning]].
> 3. Story points are usually described as covering size overall, which includes complexity, effort, and uncertainty, rather than complexity alone. The modified Fibonacci scale most teams use runs 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 20, 40, 100.
> 4. The reflog tracks where refs have pointed, so it recovers committed work. Changes you never committed are usually not in it. VS Code local history and Obsidian's own file recovery snapshots sometimes save that case, and both are worth checking before giving up.
> 5. Force pushing was safe here because a backup branch existed and this is a personal vault. On a branch anyone else has pulled, it is a different situation entirely.
> 6. Flat-fee tutoring aligns incentives, and it also moves delivery risk onto you. If a student needs three times the sessions you expected, you absorb that. That is the same tradeoff Atomic avoids by fixing the budget rather than the fee, and it is worth deciding consciously rather than by default.
