# Be a confident beginner
Meg sometimes sees you fumble through words when you are unsure instead of simply saying you do not know something. Nobody expects you to know everything, and not knowing is where the growth is. The problem is only how uncertainty comes across. Joe put it bluntly: the fumbling "blunts your intelligence," making you look less capable than you are, to the team and eventually to clients. Saying "I don't know, let me go find that answer for you," said with confidence, does the opposite.

To be clear, this is not about faking anything. Confidently saying you do not know is the honest version, not the fake one. You pushed back a little, that it is hard to say "I don't know" for the fiftieth time in one conversation when it feels like people expect you to have the answer. The reframe: at Atomic we are good at being beginners, and you will be a beginner on every new project for your whole career, even fifteen years in, because each new client brings a domain you have never seen. So get comfortable saying, plainly, "I don't know what that is, can you tell me?"

Meg added that this is a brand new experience for you but not for anyone else on the team. The Accelerator program is staffed with your level of experience in mind. What we are really asking is for you to be transparent about what you do not know yet, which takes some trust that the people around you will not hold it against you. You said you appreciated the explicit permission and will be leaning on it.

# Working with the agent
When you build with the agent, it tends to develop a mind of its own and drift from the patterns already in the app. Because you are two weeks in, you have not seen all those patterns yet, so some of your work has drifted a little. The fix is to make the pattern explicit. Ask yourself, or the agent, whether this thing exists elsewhere in the app, and tell it to copy that pattern. Stated plainly, it tends to follow.

Joe's suggestion is to bake this into a reusable [[Deliberate Practice Skill Menu|skill]] so you are not remembering to say it every time. He keeps skills for building a feature and for debugging, each starting with his development philosophy and including a standing instruction to reuse existing patterns.

# Staying inside the story, and bringing your ideas
Your energy and excitement for the work are clear, and once in a while that has led you to build things that were not in the story. Meg's ask is to stick to what the story scopes. Those boundaries usually reflect scope that was already worked out and priced with the client. Joe drew the important distinction: staying in scope does not mean switching off your creativity or product thinking. In an agentic world everyone has to think like a product developer. When you have an idea for making something better, raise it and get it into a story so it is scoped rather than a surprise to the client. Meg has already asked you to start a running list of these. The two of you will go through it and decide what joins the current story, becomes a new one, or goes to the icebox. See [[Scope Creep]].

You gave Meg a piece of feedback here, and it was a good one. The stories are sometimes only a line or two, vague enough that it is hard to know exactly what to do, like "optimize the monthly summary loading times" or a bare "jobs dashboard." You tied it partly to not yet communicating closely with her. Part of your long-term job as a consultant is taking vaguer and vaguer requests, eventually from clients who do not know what they want, and figuring out what to do. So while better-formed stories help, he does not want your only experience to be tidy ones. When a story does not make sense, the move is to go talk to whoever wrote it. See [[Handling Ambiguity]].
